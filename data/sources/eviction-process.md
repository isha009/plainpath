# The Eviction Process
> Source: General U.S. landlord-tenant principles and court self-help guidance; specific notice periods and procedures are set by state and local law.

## Eviction must go through court
In the United States, a landlord generally cannot force a tenant out on their own. They must go through a court process (often called "unlawful detainer" or "summary process"). Only a court can order an eviction, and typically only a sheriff or marshal can carry it out.

## Illegal "self-help" evictions
It is generally illegal for a landlord to remove a tenant by changing the locks, shutting off utilities (water, heat, electricity), removing the tenant's belongings, or threatening the tenant. These are called "self-help" evictions and many states let the tenant sue for damages if they happen.

## Notice comes first
Before filing, the landlord usually must give written notice — for example, a notice to pay rent or move out, a notice to fix a lease violation, or a notice that the tenancy is ending. The required notice period and wording vary by state and by the reason for the eviction.

## The tenant's right to respond
After a case is filed, the tenant is served with court papers and has a limited time to file a written response and appear in court. A tenant who responds can raise defenses (for example, the rent was paid, the unit was unsafe, or the landlord is retaliating). Tenants who ignore the papers often lose by default, so responding on time matters.
