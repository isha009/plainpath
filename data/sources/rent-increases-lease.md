# Rent Increases and Lease Basics
> Source: General U.S. landlord-tenant principles; notice periods and rent-control rules are set by state and local law.

## When rent can be raised
During a fixed-term lease (for example, a one-year lease), the rent generally cannot be raised until the term ends unless the lease itself allows it. For month-to-month tenancies, a landlord can usually raise the rent with proper written advance notice — commonly 30 days, but longer in some states and for larger increases.

## Rent control and rent stabilization
A few cities and states limit how much and how often rent can rise for covered units. Most of the country has no rent control, so whether a cap applies depends entirely on local law. A tenant should check whether their city has a rent board or stabilization ordinance.

## Reading the lease
A lease is a binding contract. Key terms to check include the rent amount and due date, the length of the term, rules on subletting and pets, who pays which utilities, and what happens at the end of the term (does it renew automatically or convert to month-to-month). Orally promised terms are hard to enforce, so important promises should be in writing.

## Ending a tenancy
To end a month-to-month tenancy, either side usually must give written notice (often 30 days). Breaking a fixed-term lease early can make the tenant responsible for rent until the unit is re-rented, though many states require the landlord to make a reasonable effort to re-rent ("mitigate damages") rather than leave it empty and charge the former tenant.
