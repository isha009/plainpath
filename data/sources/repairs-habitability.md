# Repairs and the Right to a Habitable Home
> Source: Implied warranty of habitability — general U.S. landlord-tenant doctrine recognized in most states; specifics vary by state and local housing codes.

## Implied warranty of habitability
In most states, landlords of residential housing must keep the unit fit to live in. This is called the "implied warranty of habitability." It generally covers essentials such as working heat, safe electrical and plumbing, hot and cold running water, a sound structure, and freedom from serious pest infestations.

## How to ask for repairs
Tenants should usually report needed repairs to the landlord in writing and keep a copy, with the date. Written notice creates a record showing the landlord knew about the problem, which matters if a dispute follows. Photos of the problem help too.

## Remedies if the landlord won't fix it
Depending on the state, a tenant facing a serious unaddressed problem may have options such as "repair and deduct" (paying for the repair and subtracting it from rent), rent withholding through a court, reporting to a local code-enforcement agency, or in severe cases ending the lease. These remedies have strict rules and limits that vary by state, so a tenant should confirm the local procedure before withholding rent.

## Retaliation is prohibited
Many states prohibit a landlord from retaliating against a tenant for requesting repairs, reporting code violations, or organizing with other tenants. Prohibited retaliation can include raising the rent, cutting services, or starting an eviction shortly after the tenant exercised a legal right.
