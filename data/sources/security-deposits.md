# Security Deposits
> Source: General U.S. landlord-tenant principles; specifics set by each state statute. Verify your state at your Attorney General or court self-help site.

## What a security deposit is
A security deposit is money a tenant pays at move-in that the landlord holds to cover unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear and tear. It is the tenant's money, held in trust, not an extra payment to the landlord.

## Limits and handling
Many states cap the deposit at one to two months' rent and may require the landlord to hold it in a separate account or pay interest. The exact cap, account, and interest rules vary by state — check your state statute, because there is no single federal deposit amount.

## Normal wear and tear vs. damage
Landlords generally may deduct for damage beyond normal wear and tear (for example, a broken window or large holes) but not for ordinary aging (for example, minor scuffs, faded paint, or worn carpet from normal use). Routine cleaning and repainting after a typical tenancy are often considered normal wear and tear.

## Return after move-out
Most states require the landlord to return the deposit, minus any lawful deductions, within a set number of days after the tenant moves out (commonly 14 to 30 days, but it varies by state). The landlord usually must provide an itemized list of any deductions. If the landlord wrongfully withholds the deposit, many states allow the tenant to recover the amount and sometimes additional penalties through small claims court.
